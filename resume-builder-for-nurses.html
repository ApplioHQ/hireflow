Resume Builder for Nurses

Updated 2026-07-10 · Applio
A nursing resume must lead with your active RN license, certifications, and clinical specialty because hospital recruiters and ATS software screen for exact credentials before a human ever reads it. Applio structures each of these fields so charge nurses and recruiters can verify your qualifications in seconds.

Why a nursing resume is different

Unlike most resumes, a nursing resume is screened first for hard credentials: an active, unencumbered RN license, current certifications, and specialty experience tied to a specific unit. Applicant tracking systems used by hospital networks parse for exact terms, "BSN," "CCRN," "Epic," "ICU", and rank you out if they are missing or buried.

Recruiters also read for patient acuity, unit type, and charting systems because a Med-Surg nurse and an ED nurse are not interchangeable. Your resume has to prove the right clinical fit, not just years of experience, so credentials and specialty belong at the top where both software and a hiring manager can find them fast.

What to put on a nursing resume

  • Your active RN license with the state and license number format (e.g., "RN, California, License #123456") so employers can verify it immediately via Nursys.
  • Current certifications, BLS, ACLS, PALS, and specialty credentials like CCRN, CEN, or OCN, because many units legally require them before you can work the floor.
  • Your clinical specialty and unit experience (ICU, ER, Med-Surg, L&D, PACU) since recruiters hire for a specific department, not general nursing.
  • EHR and charting systems you know, such as Epic, Cerner, or Meditech, because charting proficiency reduces orientation time and is a frequent ATS keyword.
  • Typical patient load or nurse-to-patient ratios you managed, which signals the acuity level and pace you can handle.
  • Degree and licensure path (ADN vs. BSN) plus relevant clinical rotations, especially for new grads with limited paid experience.
  • Professional memberships and affiliations like the ANA or a specialty organization (AACN, ENA) to show engagement with your field.
  • Hard nursing skills, IV insertion, ventilator management, wound care, medication administration, mapped to the job posting's requirements.

Nursing resume bullet examples

  • Managed a 1:2 patient ratio in a 24-bed surgical ICU, monitoring post-op and ventilated patients while maintaining zero central-line infections over 18 months.
  • Precepted 12 new-graduate nurses through unit orientation, contributing to a first-year retention rate above the department average.
  • Implemented a bedside shift-report protocol that helped raise unit HCAHPS communication scores by 15% across two quarters.

Frequently asked questions

How do I list my RN license on a resume?

List it prominently near the top with your credential, issuing state, and license number, for example "RN, Texas, License #123456," along with the expiration date. This lets employers verify you through Nursys before scheduling an interview.

Should a new grad nurse resume include clinical rotations?

Yes. New graduates should list clinical rotations with the setting, unit type, and hours completed, since these stand in for paid experience and show which specialties you have hands-on exposure to.

Do I need a different resume for each nursing specialty?

You should tailor it. Lead with the certifications, EHR systems, and unit experience that match the specific posting, because ATS software and charge nurses screen for specialty-specific keywords rather than general nursing experience.
